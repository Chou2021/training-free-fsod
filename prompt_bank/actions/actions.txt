## Object Class Definitions & Annotation Rules

### Attack
An "Attack" is a player attempting to hit the ball forcefully over the net, usually with an airborne leap and a raised striking arm.
Attack actions can manifest as either jumping in preparation to hit the ball or showing clear intent to strike the ball.
Annotate the player only when their body is in mid-air with only one arm extended above the head and clear intent to strike the ball.
The box should include the full visible player body. Exclude the ball unless it directly touches the player’s hand.

### Block
A "Block" is one or two players near the net reaching up with both hands raised above the head to stop or deflect an opponent’s attack.
Annotate the player whose posture most clearly shows a blocking action, especially arms and hands extended near the net.
If two players are performing a block simultaneously, select only the single most visually prominent instance.

### Defense
"Defense" is a player in a low receiving stance, often with forearms together or parallel to the floor, ready to receive the ball.
Defense also includes players in exaggerated, dynamic poses attempting to receive or save the ball.
Annotate only players clearly performing an immediate defensive receive action.
Do not annotate standing players unrelated to defense.

### Serve
A "Serve" is a player initiating play from behind the end line, typically tossing, preparing to hit, or just having contacted the ball.
When a serve is occurring, all other players on the court should be in a ready position, which can be used as supporting evidence.
Annotate the player whose pose most clearly indicates a serve action.

### Set
A "Set" is a player using fingertips to push the ball upward, usually with arms elevated, fingers spread, and body positioned under the ball.
Set actions often precede and accompany Attack actions.
Annotate the player whose hand position most clearly indicates a set.

### ball
The volleyball is the spherical game object.
Every image contains one volleyball.
Annotate the ball if it is visually localizable, even if it is small.
The box should tightly enclose the entire visible ball.
